# The Three Registers
## Apothecary, Pastoral, Iconographic — dimensions of care in *Tincture of Mercy*

---

## What this document is

This is the framework document for the three dimensions of care that structure *Tincture of Mercy*'s progression and meaning. It exists separately from the slice document and the substrate-primitives dossier because the three registers are *the moral architecture of the game* — the thing the slice translates into a teaching ramp, the thing the primitives serve, the thing the player slowly comes to recognize over the course of the pilgrimage.

This document does not yet specify mechanics in detail. It establishes the frame on its own terms first, so that mechanical decisions later can be measured against it. The slice document and the dossier will both be revised after this frame is solid; doing it the other way around risks letting mechanism dictate meaning, which is the failure mode of most RPG progression systems.

---

## The thesis

There are three registers of care. They are not a ladder. They are tools sized to different rooms.

**Apothecary** is the material register: what is wrong with the body, what plant or process can address it, what dose, what timing. It is what Kalev starts the game knowing. It is what most of the world calls "medicine." It reaches what is reachable by ingredients, instruments, and the trained hand.

**Pastoral** is the register of presence: time, touch, listening, the willingness to stay. It does not act on the body so much as it *attends to the person*. It reaches what apothecary cannot — the dying who cannot be saved, the grieving who cannot be cured, the frightened who need someone to be there. Pastoral care is not the absence of action; it is a different kind of action, one whose effect is mediated through being-with rather than doing-to.

**Iconographic** is the register of hosting: the healer becomes transparent to something that moves through him rather than from him. In the Orthodox grammar this document borrows, the iconographer does not paint *for* the holy; the holy paints *through* the iconographer. The priest at the altar does not perform the sacraments; Christ does, and the priest is the vessel. Iconographic care is what happens when a healer learns to be that kind of vessel — when the rite is not Kalev's effort but Kalev's *yielding*. It reaches what nothing else can reach: irreversible suffering, communal grief, the moment of dying, the rooms where presence and material both meet their limits.

The high-skill healer is fluent in all three. The endgame is not graduating from apothecary into something purer. The endgame is *reading the room* and reaching for the register the moment is asking for, with the wisdom to know that sometimes peace is the best possible outcome — not because peace is higher than cure but because cure was never on the table and peace was the right tool.

## First register — Apothecary

The register Kalev arrives with. It is the dominant frame at the start of the game, and the only one available in the cabin slice.

**Verbs**: gather, identify, brew, distill, prepare, administer, dose, time.

**Currency**: ingredients, recipes, Practice (P7 stat), Spirit (the resource cost of rites, P4).

**Mechanics**: the substrate primitives in their already-specified care presentation. Combat Table (P3) resolves crafting and administration. Quality Tier (P9) gates output. Vigil cooldown (P14) gates regeneration. Patient acuity vs Practice (P5) gates whether a rite *can* take.

**What it reaches**: physical illness with material causes — fevers, wounds, infections, pain that responds to molecules. The boy in Act 1, drinking water and eating porridge. The boy in Act 4, taking the febrifuge cleanly. Most of what afflicts most patients most of the time.

**What it cannot reach**: dying that is not curable. Grief. Despair. Fear. The suffering of being unattended. The mother in Act 4, who needed something Kalev did not yet know how to give. Categories of suffering that are not medical, even when they wear a medical face.

**The trap of the register**: apothecary, used alone, can convince the healer that *everything is a recipe problem*. If a patient does not respond, the answer must be a better ingredient, a higher dose, a refined method. The wise apothecary learns that some patients are past where ingredients can reach, and persistence in the apothecary frame at that point becomes a kind of refusal to see the patient. The mother's death teaches Kalev this. *He gave her what he had. There was something else she wanted.*

**The discipline of the register**: apothecary done well is craft. It rewards patience, observation, the slow accumulation of practical knowledge. It is the spine of the game's first many hours. It is not a starter zone the player graduates out of — a high-level Kalev who knows pastoral and iconographic still does most of his work apothecary-first, because most of what hurts people is in fact reachable by molecules.

## Second register — Pastoral

The first register Kalev does not start with. It unlocks through encounter — most likely through the priest at Bethany, possibly through a hospice scene or a triage where there is nothing material left to give and someone has to do *something* anyway.

**Verbs**: sit, listen, hold, witness, name, accompany, stay, return, touch, wash, sing.

**Currency**: time. Attention. The willingness to stop other tasks. The body's stillness.

**Mechanics — early thinking, not yet final**: a pastoral rite is not a single administered dose; it is a *sustained presence*. The patient's condition responds to time-spent-attending, not to a single P3 roll. Mechanically this might mean a pastoral rite is initiated by the player and then *runs* — Kalev sits with the patient for an in-game hour, during which the player can do nothing else, or can do small attendant things (refold a blanket, hold a hand, rinse a cloth) that count as continuations of the rite. The patient's grief-state, fear-state, or terminal-acceptance bar advances during the sit. When it crosses a threshold, the rite "takes." The Combat Table (P3) still rolls, but it rolls *over the duration* — multiple chances to land, with the cumulative result being the outcome.

**What it reaches**: dying-with-witness rather than dying-alone. Grief that has not yet found its bottom. Fear that needs another's presence to subside. The suffering of a child whose parent has just died. The suffering of a mother who is dying and wants someone to be in the room when it happens. The suffering of the dispossessed, the bereaved, the frightened.

**What it cannot reach**: a broken bone (apothecary). A wolf at the door (combat). A community in collective grief at a cathedral (iconographic). The pastoral register is *intimate*; it works in rooms, between persons, at the slow scale of attention.

**The trap of the register**: pastoral, used alone, can become passivity. Sitting becomes dereliction when action is what the moment requires. A child with a lacerated arm needs the wound dressed *before* she needs to be held. A man whose family is starving needs food *before* he needs to be heard. The wise pastoral healer knows when sitting is right and when sitting is escape from a harder duty.

**The discipline of the register**: pastoral done well is *sustained* attention without performance. It is not a technique to deploy; it is a quality of being-there that the patient can feel. The mechanical implementation must somehow honor this — pastoral care that the player optimizes by clicking through is pastoral care that has failed at its task. This is the hardest design problem in the whole game and the document does not yet solve it. See the open questions section.

## Third register — Iconographic

The deepest register, the one that unlocks last, and the one whose mechanics are still partly open. It is the register where Kalev learns to become a vessel — where the rite is not his act but his yielding to something that moves through him.

**Verbs (still being worked out)**: kneel, stand-still, refrain, host, host-with-naming, sing-the-hymn, light-the-lamp, hold-the-icon, write-the-name-properly, keep-the-vigil, fast, anoint. Verbs in this register tend toward *posture* and *refraining* rather than *action*. The body becomes a position. The voice becomes a hymn. The hand stops doing and starts holding.

**Currency**: this is one of the harder design questions. Apothecary costs Spirit. Pastoral costs time. Iconographic seems not to cost Kalev anything *materially* — because the work is not his — but it requires a *vessel state*. Kalev cannot host while Numbness is high; he cannot host while Pressure has not been set down; he cannot host while Burden exceeds a threshold. The "cost" is **emptying**, not spending. Iconographic care requires Kalev to have done the work of becoming hostable, which is its own kind of cost — possibly the highest cost in the game, because it requires sustained pastoral and apothecary work over many hours just to arrive at the state where iconographic is possible.

**Mechanics — early thinking, not final**: an iconographic rite likely subverts the standard P3 roll. It may not roll at all in the usual sense. Instead, it may be **gated** by the vessel state and, when the gate is open, *succeed*. The work of the player is not to perform the rite well — it is to have arrived at the moment in a hostable condition. When that condition is met, the rite's effect can exceed what apothecary or pastoral could reach: an entire room of grieving people resolved at once, a community's collective fear reduced, a death that becomes a transition rather than a tearing.

The **player verb** problem is the open one. I floated several possibilities in the conversation that produced this document:

- **Holding still**: the player must not press a button for a sustained period. Action breaks the rite.
- **Refraining**: a moment where every instinct (and tutorial-trained habit) says act, and the player must choose not to.
- **Posture-input**: a controller gesture that puts Kalev into a kneeling, standing, or facing-east position; the gesture itself is the rite, not what follows from it.
- **Naming**: writing the patient's name, the saint's name, the day, the hour, in the notebook with deliberate ink. The act of naming is the act of hosting. The notebook becomes a liturgical instrument.
- **Silence**: scenes where the player must not advance dialogue. The unbroken silence is the rite.

My instinct is that iconographic verbs will be some combination of these — different scenes calling for different gestures, with the throughline being that *iconographic care is not done by pressing buttons faster; it is done by pressing fewer of them, more deliberately, and sometimes none at all.* This will be hard to playtest. It will be even harder to communicate to a player who has been trained for thirty hours in the apothecary register's button-press idiom. Whatever solution lands here will be what makes or breaks the game's deepest moments.

**What it reaches**: the irreversible. Communal suffering. The moment of dying as a transition. Scenes where presence and material have both reached their limits and what is needed is for *something larger to be admitted to the room*. In Orthodox grammar: the Logos hosts where it is welcomed. The healer's job is to make the welcome.

**What it cannot reach**: things that demand action. A bleeding wound. A child to be fed. A wolf at the door. Iconographic care reaches *down* into what no other tool will reach; it does not reach *across* into what action is required for. Used wrongly — when bandages were what the body needed — it is spiritualizing failure into virtue, and the wise healer recognizes this as the worst betrayal the register can commit.

**The trap of the register**: making iconographic care into a power fantasy. "I prayed and the room healed" is not what this is. The register's whole shape is yielding, not wielding. If the mechanics ever make the player feel *powerful* during iconographic care, something has gone wrong. The feeling should be closer to standing in a doorway and stepping out of the way of something.

**The discipline of the register**: iconographic done well is *transparent*. The healer stops being the subject of the verb. The rite happens because the conditions are right and the vessel is willing. The credit, if there is any, is not Kalev's. This is a hard register to write and a harder register to play, and the game probably contains a relatively small number of true iconographic scenes — possibly fewer than ten across the whole pilgrimage — because each one needs to be authored with care that does not scale.

## Reading the room: the wisdom mechanic

The high-skill behavior in the late game is not picking the strongest register. It is matching register to situation. This is *the* skill the game teaches, and it is taught not by tutorial but by **outcomes**.

Every patient and every scene carries a **receptivity profile** in their data — invisible to the player, read by the engine when rites are administered. Some examples of profile shapes:

- *Apothecary primary*: most patients most of the time. Apothecary rites land cleanly. Pastoral rites land but offer little progress. Iconographic rites are wasted.
- *Apothecary then pastoral*: the boy in Bethany when Kalev finds him. The medicine has to come first to reach the body, then presence has to come second to reach the grief. Either alone is partial.
- *Past apothecary*: the mother in Act 4. Apothecary rites Glance at best. Pastoral rites land. Iconographic, if Kalev had known, would have been the truest care.
- *Pastoral primary*: a grieving relative whose body is fine. Apothecary rites Refuse — there is nothing in him to medicate.
- *Iconographic only*: rare. A community at a cathedral after a disaster. A starets dying his last. A mass grave to be consecrated.

The player learns to read these profiles by *outcome shape*. If apothecary rites Glance and Refuse on a patient, the patient may not be an apothecary case. If pastoral sitting moves the bar, the patient was a pastoral case. The Combat Table (P3) becomes a diagnostic instrument as well as a resolution instrument — the way a roll lands tells the healer something about the patient.

**Signposting through art and dialogue**: profiles are also implicitly readable through visual and narrative cues. The mother's labored breathing in Act 1 is a profile signal: she is past apothecary. The priest at Bethany describing a hospice case as "she will not eat or speak; she only watches the window" is a profile signal: this is a pastoral case. A community gathered at the cathedral with nothing to ask for, only to *be there*, is a profile signal: this is iconographic ground.

**Late-game perks**: high-Insight (P7) Kalev or specific Vocation talents may surface profile hints more directly — a faint gloss in dialogue, a subtle shift in the patient's portrait. This is power-curve at the *perception* layer, not the *power* layer. Kalev does not get stronger rites; he gets clearer eyes.

## Unlock structure: the tree as journey

The three registers come online progressively, and the talent tree's gating *is* the spiritual journey.

- **Apothecary**: open from level 1. Full 20-point tree available. Most of Kalev's early-game progression flows here.
- **Pastoral**: locked at start. Unlocks via a specific narrative gate — most likely the priest at Bethany teaching Kalev that pastoral care exists, sometime in the early-mid game (level 6–8 range). The unlock is a *moment*, not a stat threshold; it requires Kalev to witness or receive teaching that cracks his frame open.
- **Iconographic**: locked. Unlocks much later — possibly via a starets near the end of the road, possibly via a specific death-event Kalev witnesses, possibly via reaching a particular consecrated place. Probably level 18–22 in a 1–25 cap range.

**Vocation points carry over** (do not expire). Players who suspect more registers exist can save points. This rewards observation and patience — and it means the player who senses the apothecary frame is incomplete, even before the game has confirmed it, can hold points back for what comes next. That player will be playing the game at the level of register-reading the design wants to teach.

**Cross-register synergies**: some late-game talents specifically reward fluency. A talent that reads "apothecary rites administered after pastoral attendance gain +amplitude" is the kind of node that says, in mechanical language, *the body opens to medicine when the person has been attended to first*. Build several of these. The endgame fluency is fluency *across* the registers, and the talent tree should reward multi-spec.

## The risk of hierarchy: the warning

The single largest design risk in this entire framework is that the registers will accidentally read as a *ladder*: apothecary at the bottom (mundane), pastoral in the middle (deeper), iconographic at the top (highest). Every instinct of skill-tree design and every habit of game-progression rhetoric will push toward this reading. **Resist it actively.**

Concrete disciplines:

1. **Apothecary must remain viable at level 25.** A pure-apothecary build should still be able to handle most of the game's content, including the ending. The pastoral and iconographic registers expand what care can reach; they do not replace what apothecary already reaches.
2. **Iconographic must not feel powerful.** Its mechanics should produce a feeling of *yielding*, not wielding. If playtesters report feeling powerful during iconographic scenes, the implementation has failed.
3. **The narrative voice must not valorize one register over the others.** Kalev's notebook should never imply that pastoral was "better" than what he could do at the cabin, or that iconographic is the truth he's been seeking. The right framing is: *he had one tool; he learned there were others; the work is knowing when each is right.*
4. **The mother's death is not "apothecary failed."** It is "apothecary was not what she needed and Kalev did not yet know other registers existed." The distinction is everything. The first framing makes apothecary the loser; the second makes apothecary intact and Kalev's *frame* the thing that was incomplete.
5. **Cure and peace are not ranked.** Sometimes peace is the best possible outcome for a body that cannot be cured. Sometimes cure is the best possible outcome for a person who is not yet ready for peace. The wise healer does not prefer one over the other; she reaches for what the room is asking for.

If these disciplines hold, the game's moral architecture stays true. If they slip, the game becomes a pretentious version of the thing it was trying not to be.

## Where combat sits

Combat is *outside* the three registers. The registers describe Kalev's healer's tools; combat describes his survival tools. Wolves at the door are not a care problem. Brigands on the road are not a care problem. They are situations where care is not what the moment is asking for — where the moment is asking for the cudgel, the held position, the willingness to do harm to prevent worse harm.

There is one exception worth flagging: **late-game iconographic care may touch combat in rare scenes**. A man maddened by grief who has drawn a knife. A wolf-touched-with-rabies. A figure whose violence is itself a kind of suffering. In these scenes, the choice between fighting and *hosting* is real, and the iconographic register may be able to reach what the cudgel cannot. These scenes should be rare and authored with extreme care; they are not the standard. Standardly, wolves are wolves, and Kalev meets them with what he has.

## Open questions

Honest enumeration of what this document does not yet solve:

1. **What are iconographic verbs at the controller level?** This is the hardest open question. The candidates listed above (holding still, refraining, posture-input, naming, silence) are speculative. They probably need to be tested in actual scenes before any of them can be committed to.

2. **How does the pastoral unlock-narrative-gate work without feeling forced?** "The priest at Bethany teaches you" is a placeholder; the actual scene needs to feel like Kalev *recognizing* something he was already partly seeing, not like a tutorial unlock.

3. **How do you communicate "this patient needs pastoral, not apothecary" without spelling it out and ruining the wisdom-discovery loop?** Visual signposting plus outcome shape is the first answer, but it needs playtesting.

4. **How does the player understand what they're reaching for in iconographic care, given that "hosting the Logos" is a culturally specific frame that not every player will share?** The game's tone is "damaged Orthodox service book in cold Michigan light," so some specificity is in the texture already, but iconographic mechanics need to be playable by players who do not share the theology. The challenge is to make the *experience* of yielding playable as a felt thing, regardless of whether the player names what is being yielded to.

5. **What is the relationship between Burden and the registers?** Burden currently is the carried weight of others' suffering. Does pastoral care raise it more than apothecary? Does iconographic care lower it (because the work is not Kalev's)? Or does iconographic care actually raise Burden in a different way — the cost of having been a vessel? This needs working out.

6. **Does the player ever choose a specialization, or is fluency-across-registers the only viable build?** If fluency is the design goal, single-spec builds may be deliberately weaker. But that risks invalidating player choice. The likely answer is that single-spec is viable for *most* content but the deepest moments require some cross-register fluency.

These are the questions the rest of the design has to answer, and most of them are answerable only by building scenes and seeing what works.

## What this means for the slice document and the dossier

Both will need revision after this frame is solid, but the changes are translation problems, not redesign problems.

**The slice (Acts 1–5)**: Acts 1–4 stay almost identical. The only narrative change is in the Act 4 notebook entry — the line Kalev writes after the mother dies should register *bewilderment* rather than knowledge. Not "I have learned what was missing from the recipe" but something closer to *"I gave her what I had. There was something else she wanted."* That sentence is the seed of the entire pastoral and iconographic journeys.

**The Bethany payoff scene** changes more substantially. Kalev does not arrive with a corrected recipe. He arrives with a recognition — that something was missing in the cabin, that the boy may need what he could not yet give the mother. The actual cure scene is the one drafted in conversation: Kalev kneels, prepares the rite, *something stops him*, he puts the bottle down, he sits, he stays. The Pastoral register has unlocked by then through whatever earlier scene unlocked it (likely the priest at Bethany, possibly earlier on the road). The boy is healed by being attended to, with apothecary as supplement rather than spine.

**The substrate primitives dossier**: no changes to the 15 primitives themselves. The framework requires that rites and patients carry **register tags** as data, and that patients carry receptivity profiles, but these are content authoring — they do not change P1 through P15.

**One small new engine requirement**: the Combat Table resolver (P3) needs to read *register-vs-receptivity match* as a modifier on the roll. An apothecary rite on a pastoral-primary patient should produce more Refusals and Glances; a pastoral rite on the same patient should produce more Hits. This is a small modifier function on top of the existing resolver, not new architecture. Document it as an addition to P3's input parameters: `(rite, patient, attacker_skill, target_acuity, register_match_modifier) → result`.

## The closing claim

The cabin teaches the player apothecary. The road north teaches them pastoral. The cathedral, eventually, teaches them iconographic. The whole game, end to end, teaches them that none of these is enough on its own — and that the work of a healer is not to find the right register but to be fluent enough across all three to recognize what each room is asking for.

That is the moral architecture. The mechanics serve it. The substrate primitives carry it. The opening slice plants its seed in a single line: *I gave her what I had. There was something else she wanted.* Everything that follows is Kalev — and the player — learning, slowly, what that something else was, and how to give it next time.
